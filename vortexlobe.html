A propeller that unscrews through water.

Background

What is the unscrew propeller?

Traditional propellers use flat angled blades to push water backward and drive a vehicle forward. The unscrew propeller, originally designed by Robert L. Read of Public Invention, replaces those blades with four curved hemispherical lobes on a spherical rotor. When spun, the lobes push fluid axially through a rotational unscrewing motion — generating thrust without sharp cutting edges.

This is potentially safer for aquatic life. Organisms that contact a spinning lobe rotor are pushed aside rather than struck by a blade. VortexLobe builds directly on Robert's open-source design, with his knowledge and permission.

Robert Read's Original

Rotor and tail cone designed in OpenSCAD. Tested in a hot tub using a cordless drill and a wooden shaft. No standalone motor. No way to run repeatable controlled tests.

VortexLobe's Addition

A fully 3D printed motor mount designed from scratch — four iterations to get the fit right. Now the propeller runs hands-free on a 6V DC motor, enabling controlled thrust measurements at different voltages.

Why it matters

Marine propeller strikes injure and kill wildlife every year. A lobe-based propulsor with no sharp blade edges is worth investigating as an alternative for low-speed applications.

Build Guide

Assembling VortexLobe

Three printed parts, one motor, hot glue, and a battery pack. Total cost is around $11.

01

Print the three parts

Print GentlePropulsionTop, GentlePropulsionTail, and motor_mount_v4 in PLA at 0.2mm layer height, 40% infill, 3 walls, no supports. Lay each flat side down on the bed. The rotor takes 2–3 hours, the tail cone about 45 minutes, and the mount about 20 minutes.

02

Attach the motor mount to the rotor

Push the motor mount up through the square portal hole on the flat base of the rotor until the square block sits flush with the surface. Hot glue around all edges where the block meets the rotor. Let cure fully before moving on.

03

Insert the motor

Slide the 6V DC motor into the round collar from below, shaft pointing up into the portal hole. Add a drop of super glue where the shaft tip meets inside the portal hole — this is what transfers spin from the shaft to the rotor.

Let the super glue cure completely before testing. If the bond breaks under load the rotor will spin free.
04

Mount into the tail cone

Drop the full assembly — rotor, mount, and motor — into the tail cone with the motor body hanging down inside. Hot glue around the collar where it contacts the cone inner wall. This stops the motor from rotating under torque.

05

Wire up and test

Connect the two motor wires to a 4×AA battery pack. Swap the wires to reverse direction. Hold the tail cone firmly, connect power, and confirm the rotor spins cleanly. Then test in a bucket of water — tail pointing backward, rotor forward.

Experiment

Measuring thrust

A hemispherical lobe rotor will generate measurable thrust in both water and air, with thrust increasing as voltage increases.

Variables

TypeVariable
IndependentVoltage (3V, 4.5V, 6V) and medium (water vs air)
DependentThrust in grams
ControlledMotor, rotor, distance, temperature

Method

Mount VortexLobe on a kitchen scale facing down. Connect motor to voltage source. Record thrust in grams at 3V, 4.5V, and 6V. Repeat in water and in air. Calculate efficiency using:

efficiency = thrust (g) / power (W)
